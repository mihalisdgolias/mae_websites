Doctoral Programme (PhD) in Finance, Accounting and Management Science

Department of Finance, Accounting and Management Science · Faculty of Management and Economics · Cyprus University of Technology

Overview

The aim of the PhD program of the Department of Finance, Accounting and Management Science is to train PhD candidates to produce advanced research that is internationally competitive.

Based on the offered topics announced every semester, the opportunity for research is provided in the fields of Financial, Accounting, or Management Science, as well as a combination of these topics. For more information, students can contact the directors of the Doctoral Program through the Department's Secretary.

General Information

The typical duration of the curriculum is four (4) years of full-time attendance with 240 ECTS.  To obtain the PhD degree, students are expected to complete 48 ECTS of courses, pass the Comprehensive Examination (32 ECTS), prepare the PhD Dissertation Proposal (32 ECTS), and successfully complete the Research Part and the Public presentation and Defense (4x32 = 128 ECTS).

The total time of study for obtaining a doctoral degree may not exceed eight (8) academic years with a minimum of three (3) academic years.

Intended Learning Outcomes

Knowledge

  • Command a comprehensive, systematic understanding of the research literature in their chosen field of finance, accounting, or management science, and of the frontier of knowledge within it.
  • Understand advanced research methods, econometric and quantitative techniques, and theoretical frameworks relevant to capital markets, corporate finance, and related disciplines.
  • Identify open research questions and articulate how new work can contribute to the relevant body of knowledge.

Skills

  • Design and execute an original, substantial programme of research that meets standards of international scholarly quality.
  • Apply appropriate empirical and econometric methods to financial and economic data, including the analysis of large-scale and text-based datasets.
  • Critically synthesise existing literature toward the formulation of new, defensible research questions and hypotheses.
  • Communicate research findings clearly and persuasively in writing and through public presentation and defence to expert audiences.

Competences

  • Conduct research independently and with the scholarly integrity, autonomy, and rigour expected of an academic researcher.
  • Make sound, evidence-based judgements in complex and incomplete-information settings and defend them before peers and examiners.
  • Contribute original knowledge to the discipline of a quality suitable for publication in peer-reviewed outlets and for an academic or advanced research career.

Programme Structure

The programme comprises 240 ECTS over a standard duration of four years. It begins with two semesters of taught doctoral courses (theory), followed by a comprehensive examination, the submission of a research proposal, and the development, writing, public presentation, and defence of the doctoral thesis (research stages).

Course Descriptions

Mandatory Taught Courses (Theory)

FAMS 850 — Research Methods

7.5 ECTS · Mandatory · Theory

The course aims to provide the necessary training in research methods, so that students are prepared to embark on a high level of research contributing to their doctoral thesis. The course provides students the opportunity to develop the skills that are required for carrying out structured research projects by addressing research design, methodological approaches, critical literature review and statistical data analysis.

FAMS 852 — Capital Markets Topics I

7.5 ECTS · Mandatory · Theory

The goal is to discuss the latest developments in empirical research on key topics in corporate finance. A special focus will be placed on motivating and gaining insights into the empirical approaches commonly used in corporate finance research, with the hope of developing a promising research agenda for the PhD. The syllabus covers a comprehensive set of topics aimed at exposing students to the theory and methods necessary for conducting research in the field of corporate finance. The first part introduces the application of these methods through a study of the existing literature. The second part will be dedicated to the presentation and discussion of the students' research proposals.

FAMS 853 — Capital Markets Topics II

7.5 ECTS · Mandatory · Theory

This doctoral-level course examines critical theories and empirical research in corporate finance and accounting. We will delve into seminal works shaping our understanding of dividend policy, earnings management, capital structure, accounting conservatism, the burgeoning field of corporate social responsibility (CSR) and market anomalies. The course aims to equip students with a sophisticated understanding of these core concepts and their interrelationships, fostering critical analysis of contemporary research and the development of original research ideas within these areas. Students will hone their skills in evaluating methodologies, interpreting empirical findings, and formulating insightful arguments.

FAMS 854 — Empirical Corporate Finance (Empirical Financial Business Management)

7.5 ECTS · Mandatory · Theory

The primary objective of the finance workshops is to equip students with the necessary, theoretical framework in order to understand the underlying, economic underpinnings of equity selling mechanisms. The topics will include: a) the meaning of a signal as an action taken by the more informed agents that provides credible information to the less informed, b) the concept of agency costs as the conflicting interests between management and investors that can lead to suboptimal allocation of resources within the firm. Agency costs will be analyzed as a mechanism to reach equilibrium solutions in choosing optimal capital structure levels. The issues of information asymmetry and agency costs will be revisited in the context of equity issuances. Counting primarily on the recent empirical academic literature, we will review well-cited studies explaining a) the levels of underpricing, the benefits from the services of underwriting and the long-term underperformance (Initial Public Offerings), b) the discounts and the underpricing of seasoned equity offerings, the positive wealth effects and the discounts' trends in private placements of equity, the stock performance and the life cycle of Special Purpose Acquisition Companies (Seasoned Equity Offerings, Private Placements (Reg D offerings) and SPACs) and c) the timing, the stock performance and the levels of disclosure pertaining to share repurchases (Share repurchases).

FAMS 855 — Econometric Methods

7.5 ECTS · Mandatory · Theory

Much finance research is concerned with causation: whether a change in some input (for example corporate governance) causes a change in some output (for example firm performance). To provide a credible basis for causal inference a research design must address multiple econometric concerns where some of these are referred to as endogeneity. This course surveys the common methodologies used in empirical finance, including seminal papers on the field. The course is designed to help students to learn how to do empirical research in finance via lectures, literature reviews, student presentations and Stata lab applications. The purpose of this course is to provide students with a comprehensive understanding of causal inference techniques and their application in empirical research, particularly within the context of finance. Throughout the course, students will develop the ability to critically evaluate and apply advanced econometric techniques to real-world data. They will learn how to address common challenges such as endogeneity, omitted variable bias, and reverse causality, and acquire practical skills in the use of software tools like Stata for empirical analysis.

Elective Taught Courses (Theory)

FAMS 856 — Asset Valuation and Investments

7.5 ECTS · Elective · Theory · First Semester option

This module aims to provide a thorough exploration of asset pricing and valuation, merging theoretical foundations with practical applications relevant to the financial markets. It is designed to equip students with a deep understanding of a range of valuation methods, including Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) Analysis for intrinsic value estimation, Discounted Dividend Valuation for future dividend payment valuation, Price Multiples for comparative company analysis, Residual Income Valuation for equity value assessment, specific approaches for valuing private firms and considering the impact of credit risk on valuation. The course content is delivered through a combination of lectures, interactive discussions, and case studies, ensuring that students not only grasp the theoretical aspects but also how these concepts are applied in real-world scenarios. By the end of this module, students will possess the financial tools and knowledge necessary to perform comprehensive asset valuations, preparing them for a successful career in the finance industry.

FAMS 857 — Financial Business Management

7.5 ECTS · Elective · Theory · Second Semester option

The purpose of the course is to provide a comprehensive picture of Corporate Finance on a theoretical and practical level in restructuring matters. The course starts with proper valuation methods and covers issues of corporate takeovers, buyouts, collapsing / bankruptcies, etc. The course studies and develops the theory of best strategic decisions by providing the necessary knowledge on how to deal with the corporate restructuring. Furthermore, the empirical study provides an opportunity for students to undertake high-level in-depth research of an object that will be relevant and applicable to the financial industry.

FAMS 858 — Financial Models

7.5 ECTS · Elective · Theory · Second Semester option

The course aims to offer the students the required knowledges to apply financial theory in practice, using real examples and Microsoft Excel.

Admission Requirements

Eligibility — Academic Excellence

Applicants must hold a recognised university (bachelor's) degree and a postgraduate degree in Shipping, Finance, Economics, Business Administration, or in the Sciences / Applied Sciences. Applicants should have achieved very good or excellent grades in their previous programmes, and must demonstrate adequate English language proficiency.

English Language Requirements

Acceptable evidence of English language competence includes:

  • GCE / IGCSE O Level grade C or higher;
  • ELTS / IELTS 6.5 or higher;
  • TOEFL 580 (paper-based) / 82 (internet-based).

Graduates of English-language university programmes are exempt from providing an English language certificate. Greek language competence is shown via a secondary school diploma or a certificate from a higher education institution.

Innovative Research Proposal

Candidates must submit a research proposal that clearly presents a research idea or question, explains its significance, and demonstrates the expected contribution to the relevant literature.

Reference Letters

A minimum of two reference letters is required, from individuals who know the applicant academically or professionally.

Other Supporting Material

Applicants may be asked to provide additional certificates and documents, including relevant academic or professional work samples (publications, articles, research, and similar material).

Personal Interview

A personal interview is required as part of the selection process.

How to Apply

Applications and supporting documents are submitted online through the University's portal.

How You Will Learn — Research Training and Supervision

The doctoral programme is research-led. After an initial taught phase, the great majority of the degree is conducted as supervised, independent research culminating in an original thesis.

Taught Phase

During the first two semesters, candidates complete taught doctoral courses (theory) in research methods, capital markets, empirical corporate finance, and econometric methods, plus electives totalling 12 ECTS. This phase equips candidates with the methodological and theoretical foundations needed to design and execute high-quality research.

Comprehensive Examination

In the third semester, candidates sit the comprehensive examination (FAMS 800, 32 ECTS), which assesses specialised knowledge of the research literature in the candidate's field and the ability to synthesise that knowledge toward new research questions. The written examinations are coordinated by the programme coordinators together with the supervising professor.

Research Proposal

In the fourth semester, candidates prepare and submit a research proposal (FAMS 801, 32 ECTS) that defines the research question, establishes its significance, and sets out the intended contribution to the literature.

Supervised Thesis Research

From the fifth semester onward, candidates work under the guidance of their supervisor to develop the doctoral thesis (FAMS 802–805, 128 ECTS in total), culminating in the writing, submission, public presentation, and defence of the thesis.

Supervision

Each candidate is supervised within one of the Department's research areas (see the Research Areas tab). Candidates may, on the recommendation of their supervisor, enrol in additional courses relevant to their thesis specialisation.

Recognition and Career Prospects

Professional-Body Exemptions

No professional-body exemptions apply to this doctoral programme. As a research degree (PhD), it does not carry exemptions from the examinations of professional bodies.

Career Destinations

A PhD in Finance, Accounting and Management Science prepares graduates principally for research-intensive and advanced analytical careers. Typical destinations for doctoral graduates in this field include, in general terms:

  • Academia — university teaching and research positions (post-doctoral research, lectureships, and academic careers);
  • Central banks, financial regulators, and policy institutions;
  • Research roles in financial institutions, think tanks, and international organisations;
  • Advanced analytics, quantitative research, and data science roles in industry, including banking, asset management, and financial technology.

These destinations are presented in general terms as typical of doctoral graduates in finance, accounting, and management science; they are not specific placement claims for this programme.
